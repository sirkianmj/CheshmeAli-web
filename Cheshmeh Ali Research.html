About Cheshmeh Ali

Cheshmeh Ali (چشمه علی, "Spring of Ali") is one of the most significant prehistoric archaeological sites on the Iranian Plateau, with continuous and intermittent human occupation spanning approximately 8,000 years (c. 6000 BC to present). The site consists of a 7-meter-high mound covering 3.5 hectares adjacent to a permanent natural spring in the ancient city of Rey, approximately 6-14 km south of modern Tehran.

Type Site Definition

Cheshmeh Ali defines the Transitional Chalcolithic period (c. 5500-4800 BC) of the Iranian Central Plateau through its distinctive black-on-red painted pottery tradition distributed across northern Iran from Qazvin to Gorgan.

McCown 1942; Voigt & Dyson 1992

Geographic Significance

Located at the base of the Alborz mountain range, Cheshmeh Ali sits on ancient trade routes connecting Caspian coastal regions to the Central Iranian Plateau – part of what would become the Silk Road network.

Tehran Plain Settlement System

Sacred Spring

The site's permanent natural spring has been the lifeblood of settlement for millennia, with continuous sacred associations from prehistoric water worship through Zoroastrian Anahita cult to Islamic veneration.

Cultural Continuity: 6000 BC - Present

Research Significance

  • Chronological Anchor: Type site defining Transitional Chalcolithic (Cheshmeh Ali horizon, c. 5500-4800 BC) across northern Iranian Plateau
  • Exceptional Preservation: 8,000-year stratigraphic sequence with rich architectural and artifactual deposits
  • Long-Distance Connectivity: Obsidian sourcing demonstrates integration into trans-regional networks spanning 1,000+ km
  • Social Complexity: Evidence for emerging ranked society: craft specialization, long-distance trade, sedentary agricultural economy
  • Urban Origins: Part of settlement system leading to emergence of urban civilization on Iranian Plateau

Excavation History

Three major excavation campaigns have investigated Cheshmeh Ali over nearly a century, each contributing crucial data to our understanding of Iranian prehistory. Despite the site's importance, significant publication gaps remain, particularly regarding the foundational Schmidt excavations of the 1930s.

1934-1936

Erich Schmidt Expeditions

Institutions: University of Pennsylvania Museum, Boston Museum of Fine Arts, Oriental Institute (Chicago)

Director: Erich Friedrich Schmidt (1897-1964)

Area Excavated: Over 600 square meters

Major Discoveries: 173 graves documented (32 prehistoric, 124+ Islamic); established stratigraphic pottery sequence; excellent architectural preservation

Publication Status: UNPUBLISHED – comprehensive final report never released despite 90 years elapsed. Ongoing publication project by Matney, Fazeli, Pittman, Thornton (White Levy Foundation)

1998

Fazeli-Coningham Excavations

Institutions: Iranian Cultural Heritage Organization, University of Tehran, University of Bradford (UK)

Principal Investigators: Hassan Fazeli, Robin A.E. Coningham, Catherine M. Batt

Methodology: Two trenches (2m x 5m) to 10m depth; systematic radiocarbon sampling

Results: First absolute dating of site; confirmed 6th-5th millennia BC occupation

Publication: Fazeli et al. 2004. "Cheshmeh-Ali Revisited: Towards an Absolute Dating." Iran 42:13-23

2024

Shahid Beheshti University Excavations

Institution: Shahid Beheshti University Archaeological Field Research Center

Project Director: Professor Hamidreza Valipour (Faculty of Literature and Humanities, Department of Archaeology)

Deputy Director: Iman Mostafapour

Research Team: Professors and students from Shahid Beheshti University Department of Archaeology, including Kian Mansouri Jamshidi

Duration: October 1 - November 20, 2024 (7 weeks, first season)

Program: Five-year excavation program (2024-2029) established through tripartite agreement between Shahid Beheshti University, Research Institute of Cultural Heritage and Tourism (RICHT), and Cultural Heritage Directorate of Tehran Province

Research Center: Archaeological Field Research Center established in building adjacent to Tappeh Mil, Rey

Major Discoveries:

  • Extensive Sialk III Architecture: Organized village layout with multiple mudbrick residential structures (c. 4300-4000 BC, approximately 6,000-7,000 years old)
  • "Reydokht" Burial: Complete skeleton of teenage female discovered within Sialk III architectural layer, flexed position burial
  • Dating: c. 4300 BC based on stratigraphic context and associated pottery from Sialk III phase
  • Significance: First scientifically excavated prehistoric burial at Cheshmeh Ali in 26 years (since 1998)
  • First Modern Excavation: First archaeological examination of site in nearly three decades using contemporary methods

Research Objectives: Problem-oriented program to study cultural evolution of societies from Neolithic through Chalcolithic on Tehran Plain; understand architectural organization, burial practices, and daily life during transitional periods

Pending Analysis: Ancient DNA studies, isotopic analysis (diet and mobility), comprehensive radiocarbon dating, detailed architectural documentation, artifact analysis

Bioarchaeology: Skeletal analysis by Hamed Vahdati Nassab (Tarbiat Modares University) pending RICHT authorization

Publication Status: Preliminary reports published; comprehensive final report forthcoming

Research Findings

Nearly a century of research has yielded remarkable insights into prehistoric Iranian societies, from Late Neolithic village life through the emergence of social complexity in the Transitional Chalcolithic. Material culture, architecture, and burial practices document 8,000 years of human adaptation and innovation.

Cheshmeh Ali Ware Pottery

Dating: c. 5500-4800 BC (Transitional Chalcolithic)

Characteristics: High-fired black-on-red painted ceramics with geometric designs (chevrons, hatched patterns) and rare animal motifs (ibex, goats)

Production: Local manufacture (geochemical analysis 2008) with shared stylistic traditions across 500km

Museum Collections: Metropolitan Museum, LACMA, British Museum, Oriental Institute Chicago, National Museum of Iran

Defining Ceramic Tradition of Northern Iran

Lithic Technology & Trade

Obsidian Sources: XRF analysis (MURR 2021) of 9 artifacts revealed 8 specimens from Turkey and Armenia sources

Distance: Exchange networks spanning 1,000+ km during Transitional Chalcolithic

Interpretation: Integration into trans-regional trade systems connecting Iranian Plateau with Anatolian and Caucasian regions

Academia 2021; Schmidt Collection, UPenn Museum

Architecture & Settlement

Schmidt Era: Excellent preservation noted but plans never published

2024 Discovery: Extensive Sialk III residential architecture (c. 4300-4000 BC) – organized village layout with multiple mudbrick structures

Significance: First systematic documentation of Chalcolithic domestic architecture at site

Status: Detailed architectural analysis and publication pending

Valipour & Mostafapour 2024

Burial Practices & Bioarchaeology

Schmidt Burials: 173 total graves (32 prehistoric, 124+ Islamic) – burial data compilation ongoing (Gustavel 2020)

"Reydokht" Discovery (2024): Female skeleton, flexed position, c. 4300-4000 BC within Sialk III architectural layer

Pending Analysis: Ancient DNA, isotopic studies (diet, mobility), radiocarbon dating

Significance: First scientifically excavated prehistoric burial in modern era

Analysis: Hamed Vahdati Nassab, Tarbiat Modares Univ

Chronological Framework

  • Late Neolithic (6200-5500 BC): Chaff-tempered ceramics; earliest sedentary occupation
  • Transitional Chalcolithic (5500-4800 BC): Type site – Cheshmeh Ali ware; long-distance trade; emerging social complexity
  • Sialk III (4300-4000 BC): Organized village architecture; burial practices; domestic activities
  • Major Abandonment (c. 3000 BC): Sudden cessation; cause unknown
  • Late Bronze Age Gap (1900-1500 BC): No occupation at mound (nearby Rashkan occupied)
  • Parthian (3rd c BC - 3rd c CE): Major reoccupation; defensive wall; Anahita temple
  • Islamic Periods (7th c CE - present): Sacred spring; 124+ medieval burials; Qajar rock relief

Research Team

Archaeological research at Cheshmeh Ali involves collaboration between Iranian institutions, international universities, and specialized laboratories. The current excavation project (2024-2029) is led by Shahid Beheshti University's Archaeological Field Research Center under Professor Hamidreza Valipour, building on nearly a century of scholarly investigation.

Shahid Beheshti University Archaeological Field Research Center

Establishment: 2024, through tripartite agreement between Shahid Beheshti University, Research Institute of Cultural Heritage and Tourism (RICHT), and Cultural Heritage Directorate of Tehran Province

Location: Building adjacent to Tappeh Mil, Rey, Tehran Province

Mission: Five-year problem-oriented excavation program (2024-2029) to study cultural evolution from Neolithic through Chalcolithic periods on Tehran Plain

Leadership: Professor Hamidreza Valipour (Director), Iman Mostafapour (Deputy Director)

First Season: October 1 - November 20, 2024 (7 weeks)

Key Achievement: First archaeological examination of Cheshmeh Ali in nearly three decades; discovery of "Reydokht" burial and extensive Sialk III architecture

Collaboration: Faculty and graduate students from Department of Archaeology, with specialized analysis partnerships including Tarbiat Modares University (bioarchaeology), international laboratories (aDNA, isotopic studies)
